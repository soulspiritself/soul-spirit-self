Teresa wrote *The Interior Castle* in 1577, at the age of sixty-two, under direct command from her Carmelite superiors who wanted a definitive account of her teaching on prayer. She finished the book in five months while travelling, founding new convents, and arguing simultaneously with the Inquisition. It is the most considered statement of her contemplative knowledge.

The central image is a castle of crystal divided into seven concentric mansions, with God dwelling in the innermost chamber. The soul begins outside the castle entirely, in dispersion and distraction. The contemplative path is the slow movement inward, mansion by mansion, each requiring its own particular kind of work and surrender. The first three mansions are the territory of ordinary devotion. The fourth introduces the *prayer of quiet*, the first taste of contemplation as gift rather than effort. The fifth, sixth, and seventh describe progressively deeper states of union, culminating in the spiritual marriage that is Teresa's mature account of nondual recognition in Christian terms.

What distinguishes the *Castle* from earlier maps of the path is Teresa's psychological precision. She describes specific contemplative states with the matter-of-fact tone of someone reporting weather: how it begins, how long it tends to last, what to expect when it ends, what to do if a particular state has not yet come, what to do if it has come and gone. She is candid about her own confusions and the moments she misled herself.

## First mansions

### Chapter one
I began to think of the soul as if it were a castle made of a single diamond or of very clear crystal, in which there are many rooms, just as in heaven there are many mansions. For if we reflect upon it, sisters, the soul of the just is nothing but a paradise, in which, the Lord tells us, He takes His delight.

### Chapter two
You must know that all the harm which comes to a soul does not come from outside it, from the world, but from itself. The soul that does not enter into itself, and recognise its own dignity, can never know God.
