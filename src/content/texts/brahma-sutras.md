## Opening of the inquiry

### I.1.1
*Sutra:* Then, therefore, the inquiry into Brahman.

*Shankara's commentary:* The word "then" indicates that something has preceded. The subject of inquiry is Brahman, of whom no other definition is possible than that he is the source from which proceeds the origination, subsistence, and dissolution of this world. By the word "therefore" the connection is shown between this inquiry and that which has preceded. The inquiry is undertaken because the result, the knowledge of Brahman, is the highest aim.

### I.1.2
*Sutra:* That from whence the origin of this world proceeds.

*Shankara's commentary:* Brahman is that omniscient, omnipotent cause from which proceed the origin, subsistence, and dissolution of the world. The world that we see, ordered, full of distinct beings and diverse purposes, cannot have been produced by anything insentient, any blind material cause. Such a world demands an intelligent source.

## On the nature of Brahman

### I.1.4
*Sutra:* But that Brahman is the cause of the world, and the topic of all the Vedanta texts.

*Shankara's commentary:* The Vedanta texts, when carefully considered, will be seen to refer to Brahman as their main topic. To say that one Brahman is the topic of all Vedanta-texts and to find that all the texts harmonise in their teaching, is to find them harmonising in the doctrine of one all-knowing, all-powerful Brahman.

## On the Self

### II.3.18
*Sutra:* The individual soul is not a portion of Brahman, but Brahman itself, conditioned by the limiting adjuncts.

*Shankara's commentary:* The individual soul is not different from the supreme Self in essence. It only appears different by reason of its limiting adjuncts. When the limiting adjuncts of body and mind are seen as what they are, the soul stands forth as Brahman.
