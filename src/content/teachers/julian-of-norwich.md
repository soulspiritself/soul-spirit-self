Julian of Norwich is the first woman known to have written a book in English. Almost nothing is recorded of her outer life beyond the few facts she gives in her own text. She was born around 1342, lived through the Black Death and the Peasants' Revolt, and at the age of thirty fell into a serious illness during which she experienced sixteen visions of the suffering Christ and the love of God.

She survived the illness, was professed as an anchoress at the church of St Julian in Norwich (the source of the only name we have for her), and spent the rest of her life in a small cell attached to the church, available through a window for spiritual counsel to anyone who came. The book we have, *Revelations of Divine Love*, exists in two versions: a shorter text written soon after the visions, and a longer text she revised and expanded over the next twenty years.

Her central insight is that love is the meaning of everything. After two decades of pondering what was shown to her, she heard the answer in her interior: *Wouldst thou learn thy Lord's meaning in this thing? Learn it well: love was His meaning. Who shewed it thee? Love. What shewed He thee? Love. Wherefore shewed it He? For Love.* Her famous formulation, *all shall be well, and all shall be well, and all manner of thing shall be well*, is not optimism but a metaphysical claim: the ground of being is love and works only love, even where this is hidden from us.

Julian was nearly unknown for centuries. Her book survived in a handful of manuscript copies, several of them made by exiled English Benedictine nuns in the seventeenth century, was first printed in 1670, and reached a wide readership only with Grace Warrack's 1901 modernisation. She now anchors the English contemplative tradition.

## The teaching

The hazelnut comes in the first revelation. Julian is shown a small round thing lying in the palm of her hand, the size of a hazelnut, and wonders what it could be. The answer comes: *it is all that is made*. Everything that exists, seen from the side of its maker, is this small. She marvels that something so little does not simply fall into nothing, and is answered that *it lasteth, and ever shall, for that God loveth it*. From the showing she draws three properties, repeated like a refrain through the book: God made it, God loveth it, God keepeth it.

The image carries her whole theology in miniature. Creation is tiny against the love that holds it, and the holding never stops, which is the ground of her confidence that all shall be well. The practical counsel she attaches is equally compact. The soul finds no rest in things so small; it settles only when it loosens its grip on created things as final goods and rests in the love that holds them.
