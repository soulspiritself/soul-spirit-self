Almost nothing is known of Hadewijch's outer life. She was a Beguine, probably from Brabant in what is now Belgium, writing in Middle Dutch in the middle of the thirteenth century. She seems to have led a Beguine community for a time, was eventually forced out by ecclesiastical pressure, and may have spent her later life as a wanderer.

What survives is her writing, all of it preserved in a small handful of fourteenth-century manuscripts: thirty-one letters of spiritual counsel, fourteen visions described in close detail, and forty-five poems and stanzaic poems in courtly love verse. Together they form one of the most refined literary corpora of medieval mysticism.

Her central concept is *Minne*, a word that translates roughly as Love but carries the full weight of medieval courtly love poetry. Minne is the divine love that draws the soul, that wounds it, that demands everything. Hadewijch describes the soul's relation to Minne with the precision of a courtly lover and the rigour of a theologian. The path is not easy. Minne strips away every consolation. The soul learns to love without reward, without felt presence, without anything but the bare orientation of love itself.

There is no public-domain English translation. The standard scholarly version is the Paulist Press *Classics of Western Spirituality* volume translated by Mother Columba Hart (1980), which gathers the *Letters*, *Visions*, and *Poems in Couplets*. Selections also appear in Bernard McGinn's *The Flowering of Mysticism* and in anthologies of Beguine writing.
